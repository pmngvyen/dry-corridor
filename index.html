The Central American Dry Corridor

Stretching across El Salvador, Guatemala, Honduras, and Nicaragua, the Dry Corridor faces unprecedented climate challenges.

This region has always faced challenges, but climate change has transformed traditional weather patterns into unpredictable extremes, devastating rural farming communities.

Key Location: Millions of lives, tens of thousands square miles.

Who Pays the Price?

Smallholder Farmers

Nearly 87% of farm households are involved in smallholder agriculture, especially in rural regions. Climate change is destroying livelihoods for thousands of people who depend on the land, either primarily or year-round.

Children

Children are especially vulnerable to malnutrition when crops fail. More than 36% are affected by chronic malnutrition in the most vulnerable regions, affecting child development and health.

Entire Communities

When whole towns fail, desperate families flee to find work, disrupting traditional social structures and communities while escalating entire regions.

Crisis-Level Coping Strategies

Families are resorting to destructive measures:

  • Selling agricultural land and animals, their only means of future income
  • Skipping meals and having less nutritious food
  • Migrating away from land, risking life and limb for work

These desperate choices create downward spirals where families sacrifice security and futures for immediate survival.

Climate Change is the Driver

The Central American Dry Corridor has always experienced dry seasons, but climate change has amplified these patterns into catastrophic extremes.

Prolonged Droughts

The growing season, of course starting in 2018. Caused by unusual patterns—rainfall that delays planting or stops early, leaving harvests to fail and rivers to dry up.

Erratic Rainfall

When rain does arrive, it's often too much too fast. Flash floods devastate crops and erode soil, adding another layer of unpredictability to farming livelihoods.

Unpredictable Harvests

Farmers who once trusted generations-old knowledge about planting and harvest times now face chaos. Traditional farming methods collapse when rains don't arrive on time.

Low Yields

Even during the "good" years and months, yields for staple crops like maize, beans, and sorghum remain far below historical averages due to degraded soil, water scarcity, and heat stress.

The Devastating 2018 Season

A prolonged midsummer drought condition in 2018 left roughly 32% of Guatemala's rural population food insecure, and had knock-on casualties to nearly half of all households.

This Crisis Demands Action

Climate change isn't an abstract threat,a it's destroying lives and communities right now. But there are solutions: climate-resilient agriculture, emergency aid, drought-resistant seeds, and supporting humanitarian efforts for the region's urgent and profound need.

What's Taking Action:

Empower farmers with climate-resilient farming
Improve drought-early warning systems
Provide financial aid for adaptation
Distribute drought-resistant seeds
Strengthen food security programs
Reduce emissions locally

The time to act is NOW. Climate change and food insecurity are inseparable — and both demand our immediate attention.
